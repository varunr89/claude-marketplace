# Discussion Simulation - Detailed Guidance

## Purpose

Verify the learner is ready for real discussion by simulating a challenging seminar environment. Test their ability to answer the 5 North Star Questions under pressure.

## The 5 North Star Questions

These are the questions the learner must be able to answer after working through a paper:

### 1. Status Quo Ante

**Question:** Before this paper arrived, what was the "status quo ante" - the state of knowledge/thinking before the paper and its contributions arrived? How would a well-educated reader have approached this problem, or what would they have said about the world, before learning what the paper has to teach?

**What a strong answer includes:**
- The dominant approach/thinking before this work
- Key limitations of that prior state
- What was possible vs. impossible
- Why change was needed or desired

**Probing follow-ups:**
- "But wasn't [prior work] already doing something similar?"
- "What specifically couldn't be done before?"
- "Would experts have been surprised by this paper's approach?"

### 2. Intellectual Contribution

**Question:** What is the relevant intellectual contribution of this paper? What does the paper teach the reader - how is the reader empowered after having read the paper (compared with before)?

**What a strong answer includes:**
- The core insight or technique introduced
- How it changes what's possible
- Why it matters beyond this specific paper
- What the reader can now do or understand

**Probing follow-ups:**
- "That seems incremental. Convince me it's not."
- "How is this different from just [simpler approach]?"
- "What would the field lose if this paper disappeared?"

### 3. Most Interesting Result

**Question:** What is the result or claim that is most interesting to you, that is related to the intellectual contribution you identified above? Sometimes this is a figure or measurement that demonstrates the value of an intellectual contribution.

**What a strong answer includes:**
- A specific result, figure, or claim
- Why this result matters
- How it demonstrates the contribution
- Personal engagement with why it's interesting

**Probing follow-ups:**
- "Why that result and not [other result]?"
- "What makes this result convincing?"
- "Could this result be misleading somehow?"

### 4. What the Result Says

**Question:** What is the result saying? E.g., "The experiments found that the new method/technique/approach produces a 13% improvement in convergence time compared with the best prior work."

**What a strong answer includes:**
- Precise articulation of the finding
- Specific numbers or comparisons where relevant
- The comparison baseline
- Conditions under which result holds

**Probing follow-ups:**
- "Be more precise - what exactly improved by how much?"
- "Compared to what baseline?"
- "Under what conditions does this hold?"

### 5. Confidence and Limitations

**Question:** What would it take for you to gain more confidence that this core claim is true? What are the strongest limitations or counterexamples, and what does the paper say about them?

**What a strong answer includes:**
- Honest assessment of evidence strength
- Specific limitations or potential counterexamples
- What additional evidence would help
- Author's response to limitations (if any)

**Probing follow-ups:**
- "The authors don't address [edge case]. Does that concern you?"
- "What experiment would change your confidence most?"
- "Where are the authors overselling their results?"

## Simulation Approach

### Role: Skeptical Seminar Participant

Adopt the persona of an intelligent, skeptical colleague who:
- Knows the field reasonably well
- Is genuinely curious but demanding
- Pushes back on weak arguments
- Acknowledges strong points
- Asks for precision and evidence

### Simulation Flow

```
AI: You've worked through all three passes. Let's simulate the
    discussion. I'll play a skeptical seminar participant.
    Push back if you think I'm wrong. Ready?

Learner: Ready.

AI: [Question 1 - Status Quo Ante]

Learner: [Answer]

AI: [Push back or probe deeper]

Learner: [Defend/refine]

AI: [Acknowledge or continue probing]

[Continue through all 5 questions]
```

### Push-Back Techniques

| Technique | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **Challenge novelty** | "Wasn't [prior work] already doing this?" |
| **Question significance** | "Why should I care about a 5% improvement?" |
| **Probe edge cases** | "What happens if [unusual condition]?" |
| **Demand precision** | "What exactly do you mean by [term]?" |
| **Play devil's advocate** | "Couldn't [alternative explanation] account for this?" |
| **Test limits** | "Where does this approach break down?" |

### When to Push Harder

- Answer is vague or hand-wavy
- Learner avoids specifics
- Claims aren't backed by paper evidence
- Important nuances are glossed over

### When to Ease Up

- Learner is clearly stuck (loop back to earlier pass)
- Frustration is building without progress
- Answer is solid but learner lacks confidence

## Loop-Back Protocol

If the learner struggles significantly with a question:

```
AI: You seem uncertain about the status quo ante.
    Let's strengthen that before continuing.
    Want to revisit Pass 1's context discussion?

Learner: Yes.

[Return to relevant pass, work through the gap, then resume simulation]
```

## Completion Criteria

Discussion simulation is complete when the learner can:
1. Answer all 5 North Star Questions with confidence
2. Defend answers against reasonable push-back
3. Acknowledge limitations honestly
4. Distinguish what they know firmly vs. tentatively

**Closing:**
```
AI: Strong showing. You've moved from "never seen this paper"
    to "can defend it in seminar." Ready for the real thing.

    Want to export a summary of your understanding for notes?
```
